Georgia → Germany

Kiko.

A street dog from Tbilisi. Raised by a whole neighbourhood. Ready to love you — just give her a minute.

Her Story

From a cold corner of Tbilisi to a family that was waiting for her. Every stop along the way made her who she is.

Chapter 01 — Found

New Year's Eve, a Hill, and Eight Puppies

Kiko was born into a pack of about 12 street dogs — a family that deeply distrusted humans and was scared of everything. Just before New Year, we found 8 puppies on a hill. On January 1st, in the rain, we dragged a doghouse up the slope. The puppies looked at it with complete indifference and decided not to live in it.

The neighbours threw rocks at them. People fed them salad, aubergine with mayonnaise, and whatever else was around. So we built an enclosure — brought doghouses, piles of cushions, blankets, and warm food twice a day. Kiko was the curious one. The playful one. The one who was always happy to see us, even when she didn't trust us yet.

Chapter 02 — Winter

The Whole Winter, Together

All winter we came every day — warm water, hot meals, flea treatment, and slow attempts at making friends. They weren't interested in being friends. But we kept showing up anyway.

Kiko and her siblings were vaccinated — which required heroic levels of catching. Then, in spring, we took Kiko, Bubka, and Ponyo to be spayed and started looking for homes for them.

Chapter 03 — Surgery

One Cone, Zero Drama

She came through surgery calmly and without complaint. Spayed, chipped, vaccinated, and ready for the next chapter. The vet said she was in great shape. Kiko seemed to already know that.

Chapter 04 — Foster Home

2.5 Months That Changed Everything

That's how Kiko came to stay with us — me, my partner, and our dog Fuji. She lived with us for two and a half months. Sometimes easy, sometimes not. Always worth it.

We fell for her completely. And you will too.

First Days

She Was Scared.
But She Tried.

"She found a safe spot under the table and didn't come out for several days. We couldn't approach her, let alone touch her. She was very thin — she'd been too shy to steal food from the others in the enclosure. The first one she trusted wasn't us. It was Fuji."

How She Learned
to Trust

Trust didn't come all at once. First there had to be a dog. Then, slowly, there could be people.

Stage 1

First, Fuji

Fuji — our dog — was the one she chose first. She watched him like an older, very confident brother, followed him everywhere, and copied everything he did. Through him, she started to understand that this home was safe. That the people in it were safe too.

Stage 2

Then, Us

She started by taking food from our hands — grab and run. Then she began exploring the flat on her own terms: one room, then another, then the kitchen, then the whole apartment. After a month she was walking freely around the house, sleeping soundly, obeying perfectly. We kept asking ourselves: how is she this good? She's a puppy.

Then one day she just came over, lay down next to us, and let herself be hugged. That was it. We were done for.

The Good Stuff

Kiko Being Kiko

These are not quirks. These are features. You'll love every single one.

Lying in the sun, tracking its movement across the flat like a very small satellite dish.

Deer antler. Hours of focused, very serious work.

Will clean a yoghurt pot with surgical precision.

Her sleeping positions are art. Document them all.

Balcony supervisor. Watching the neighbourhood. Very thorough.

The Textile Collection

Socks, sleep masks, harnesses, shoelaces — if it's fabric and unattended, she'll carry it to her spot. Not to destroy. Just to have. You'll find things in interesting places.

The Cucumber Fan

Loves cucumber. Loves tomatoes. Is suspicious of strawberries. Has no opinion on apples. Has very strong opinions about cottage cheese containers.

The Sunspot Migration

She follows the sunlight as it moves across the apartment throughout the day. It is a whole routine. She knows exactly where it will be next.

The Sleep Touch

She likes to fall asleep touching a person. Just a paw on your foot, or a nose against your leg. She doesn't need much. Just to know you're there.

The Couch Philosopher

Loves lying on the sofa and thinking. About what, exactly? Unknown. But it looks very important.

The Enthusiastic Eater

She eats fast. Very fast. Use a slow feeder bowl — it helps, and it'll give her brain something to do at the same time.

Still a Work
in Progress

She's not finished. Neither are any of us. Here's what to expect and what to keep working on together.

What She's Still Learning

Brooms, mops, vacuums

Still scared. Introduce slowly, let her investigate on her own terms. Don't force it.

Unfamiliar dogs and people

She warms up — it just takes a bit. Don't rush introductions. Let her sniff and observe first.

Eating too fast

Use a slow feeder bowl. She'll eat from it happily — it also keeps her brain busy.

"Come" command

Knows it at home. Still building it in new environments and outdoors.

New objects

She approaches cautiously and on her own schedule. Just leave new things out — she'll investigate when she's ready.

Eating off the ground

Works on it. A "leave it" command helps — she knows it, just needs consistent reinforcement.

Toileting on the move

She sometimes goes in the middle of the pavement. Still learning to find a better spot. Patient praise helps.

Good to Work On with a Trainer

Fear of unfamiliar things

A trainer can build a structured desensitization plan — brooms, vacuums, new people, new places. She responds very well to games and rewards.

"Come" in new environments

She knows the command. Building it in parks and on the street with consistent proofing will make it solid fast.

Loose leash on a short lead

She doesn't love the short leash yet. A trainer who uses positive reinforcement will help her associate it with good things.

General confidence building

Enrichment, nose work, puzzle feeders — anything that lets her explore and succeed on her own. She blossoms with it.

Finally

Hi.
I'm Kiko.

Hi. I'm Kiko. I was born on a hill in Tbilisi, in a pack that didn't trust anyone. For a long time, neither did I.

Then some people kept showing up — every day, rain or not, with warm food and zero agenda. Then there was a dog named Fuji who showed me, very patiently, that home is a real thing. Then there were two humans who sat very still and let me decide when I was ready. I took my time. They waited.

I've heard there's a family in Germany who's been getting ready for me for a while now. They've been nervous and excited and have thought about me a lot. I think that's very sweet. I've been thinking about them too, in my own way — from the balcony, mostly, watching the street and wondering what comes next.

I'll need a little time to find my spot in your home, learn your sounds, figure out the routine. I'm not slow — I'm careful. There's a difference. Once I trust you, I will follow you everywhere, sleep touching your leg, and carry your socks to my bed as a gift. That's just how I show love.

— Kiko, Tbilisi → Germany
